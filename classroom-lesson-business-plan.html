Why Write a Business Plan?

A business plan is a written document that describes your business, its goals, and how you plan to achieve them. You need it to: clarify your own thinking before spending money, pitch to investors or lenders, apply for grants (like Tony Elumelu Foundation), and track your progress against goals. You do not need a 50-page document - a clear one-page plan is enough for most small businesses starting out.

The One-Page Business Plan

A practical one-page business plan covers six sections: (1) Business Overview: What is your business? What problem does it solve? Who are your customers? (2) Product or Service: What exactly do you sell or provide? What makes it better than alternatives? (3) Target Market: Who specifically will buy from you? Where are they? How many are there? (4) Revenue Model: How do you make money? What is your price? How many customers do you need to cover costs? (5) Costs and Budget: What does it cost to start and operate monthly? What are your fixed and variable costs? (6) Goals: What are your targets for Month 1, Month 3, and Month 12?

Financial Basics: Revenue, Costs, and Profit

Three numbers every entrepreneur must understand: Revenue = Price x Quantity sold (the money coming in). Costs = Fixed costs (rent, salaries - same every month) + Variable costs (materials, packaging - change with sales volume). Profit = Revenue minus Total Costs. If costs exceed revenue, you are losing money. Break-even point = the sales volume at which revenue equals costs and you are not losing money. Calculate your break-even before starting.

Common Business Plan Mistakes

Overestimating revenue: Be conservative - assume sales will start slowly. Underestimating costs: Account for ALL costs including transport, packaging, phone credit, and your own time. No market research: Ask 10-20 potential customers if they would buy and at what price before writing your plan. No competitive analysis: Know who else solves this problem and why customers would choose you. Ignoring legal requirements: Know what permits, registrations, or licenses your business needs in Liberia.

💡 Key Tips

  • Write the financial section last - once you understand your costs and pricing clearly.
  • Research actual prices for supplies and equipment before estimating costs.
  • Be honest about the time it will take to become profitable - most businesses take 6-18 months.
  • Review and update your business plan quarterly as you learn what actually works.

📝 Practice Assignment

Write a one-page business plan for a real or imaginary business you could start in Liberia. Cover all 6 sections: Business Overview, Product or Service, Target Market, Revenue Model, Costs and Budget, and 3-month Goals. Be specific with numbers: a price, an estimated number of customers per month, and estimated monthly costs. Share it with a trusted friend or mentor and get their honest feedback.
