⚠️ Understanding Cyber Threats

Cybercrime is one of the fastest-growing threats in West Africa. Every day, Liberians lose money to phone scams, fake job offers, SIM swap fraud, and phishing attacks. This lesson teaches you to recognize the most common cyber threats targeting Liberians, understand how attackers think, and protect yourself before you become the next victim.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand: The main types of cyber threats active in Liberia and West Africa
  • Understand: The most common online and phone-based scams targeting Liberians
  • Understand: How to identify a phishing attempt before clicking or responding
  • Understand: What to do immediately if you are targeted by a cyber threat

Section 1 — Cybercrime in Liberia: Why You Are a Target

Cybercrime in West Africa has grown dramatically in the past decade, fueled by increased internet access, mobile money adoption, and sophisticated criminal networks. In Liberia, the Liberia Telecommunications Authority (LTA) has reported thousands of fraud cases related to mobile money scams, SIM swap attacks, and impersonation fraud annually. Globally, developing countries are disproportionately targeted because awareness is lower, law enforcement capacity is still developing, and mobile money systems — while enormously valuable — have created new attack surfaces for criminals.

Critically, you do not need to be wealthy or tech-savvy to be a target. Criminals target ordinary Liberians receiving remittances from abroad, market traders using MTN MoMo or Lonestar Cell mobile money, students with smartphone access, and anyone with a Facebook or WhatsApp account. The attackers are not random; they are organized, patient, and experienced at exploiting human psychology in ways that feel convincing even to intelligent, careful people.

Understanding how these attacks work is the most important defense. Cybercriminals rely on urgency, fear, and confusion — once you understand their methods, their attacks lose their power. Knowledge is not just helpful in this context; it is the difference between losing your life savings and protecting everything you have worked for.

Section 2 — Types of Cyber Threats

Cyber threats come in several forms. Understanding each type helps you recognize attacks when they happen. The categories below cover the threats most frequently reported in Liberia and across West Africa.

Phishing

Fraudulent emails, SMS messages, or social media messages designed to trick you into revealing personal information (passwords, PIN numbers, account details) or clicking a malicious link. Example: "Your MTN MoMo account has been suspended. Click here to verify your identity." The link takes you to a fake website that steals your login credentials. Phishing messages are crafted to look authentic — they may use official logos, familiar language, and urgent tone to bypass your natural caution.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone calls where the attacker pretends to be from a bank, mobile money provider, government agency, or international organization. They create urgency: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours unless you verify your PIN now." The caller may know your name, partial account details, or other personal information to seem legitimate. No legitimate organization will ever ask for your PIN over the phone — not a bank, not MTN, not the government.

SIM Swap Fraud

The attacker contacts your mobile network provider, pretending to be you, and requests your phone number be transferred to a new SIM card they control. Once they have your number, they can receive your mobile money OTP (one-time password) codes and drain your account. This is one of the most damaging scams in Liberia. Victims often discover the attack only when their phone suddenly loses all signal — by which time the attacker may have already accessed their mobile money account.

Malware and Viruses

Malicious software installed on your device through suspicious downloads, fake app stores, infected USB drives, or malicious links. Malware can log your keystrokes, steal your passwords, or even lock your device until you pay a ransom (ransomware). Fake versions of popular apps — banking apps, WhatsApp clones, "free" games — are a common delivery method in West Africa. Only install apps from official stores (Google Play, Apple App Store) and avoid clicking download links in messages.

Social Engineering

The broadest category — any manipulation technique that exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. This includes pretending to be a friend in need of emergency money, creating fake romantic relationships to extract money (romance scams), or impersonating authority figures such as police officers, immigration officials, or UN representatives. Social engineering is effective because it targets human emotions: compassion, fear, greed, and trust. No security software can fully protect against it — awareness is the only reliable defense.

Section 3 — Common Scams in Liberia

The "You Won a Prize" Scam: You receive an SMS, WhatsApp message, or Facebook notification saying you have won a cash prize, phone, or scholarship. To claim your prize, you must pay a small "processing fee" or "tax" upfront. No prize ever arrives. The fee is the entire point. These messages are sent to thousands of people simultaneously; the scammers profit from the small percentage who pay. Legitimate competitions never ask you to pay to receive your winnings. If you did not enter a competition, you cannot have won it.

Fake Job Offers: A message arrives on WhatsApp, Facebook, or by SMS offering an unusually well-paying job — data entry from home, an overseas position, a government contract — with minimal requirements. To proceed, you must pay a "registration fee," "background check fee," or "visa processing fee." These jobs do not exist. Real employers do not ask job seekers to pay fees for job applications. If a job offer asks you to send money before you can start work, it is a scam without exception.

Romance Scams: Someone connects with you on Facebook or WhatsApp, often claiming to be a Liberian working abroad, a UN worker, or a foreign professional. They build a relationship over weeks or months — sending messages, sometimes photos or even video calls — then request money for an emergency: medical bills, a stuck shipment, a flight home to meet you. The person is often not who they claim to be. Profile photos may be stolen from real people's social media accounts. These scams can last months and cause serious financial and emotional harm. Be cautious of any online relationship that moves quickly and eventually involves a financial request.

Government Impersonation: An attacker poses as a government official, police officer, immigration officer, or court representative and threatens you with arrest, deportation, or legal action unless you pay a fine immediately via mobile money. The threat is designed to cause panic and override rational thinking. Real government officials do not collect fines or fees via mobile money. If you receive such a call, hang up immediately and verify through official channels — call the relevant agency's publicly listed number directly, never a number provided by the caller.

Section 4 — How to Spot a Phishing Attempt

Phishing messages are designed to trigger panic and quick action before you have time to think critically. The attacker wants you to act NOW — click this link, enter your PIN, confirm your account — before your rational mind can evaluate whether the request makes sense. The most powerful defense is to slow down. Any message demanding immediate action with threats of account suspension, arrest, or loss of money should immediately raise your suspicion. Examine the message carefully before doing anything.

Recognizing phishing is a skill you can develop. Once you know what to look for, fraudulent messages become much easier to identify. The following red flags appear in the vast majority of phishing attempts. The more of these you see in a single message, the more certain you can be that it is fraudulent.

  • Urgent language: "Act now or your account will be deleted in 24 hours"
  • Requests for your PIN, password, or OTP code — legitimate organizations NEVER ask for these
  • The sender's email address or phone number does not match the official organization (e.g., MTNMoMo@gmail.com instead of an official MTN domain)
  • Links that look slightly wrong: mtn-momo-liberia.com instead of mtn.com.lr
  • Prizes, offers, or opportunities that seem too good to be true
  • Poor grammar, spelling errors, and unprofessional language
  • Pressure to complete the action using WhatsApp or a personal phone rather than official channels
  • Request to keep the interaction secret ("don't tell family members about this offer")

Section 5 — What To Do If You Are Targeted

If you receive a suspicious message or call: do not click any links, do not call back numbers provided in the message, and do not share any personal information. Hang up on calls demanding immediate action. Block the number. Trusting your instincts is important — if something feels wrong, it very likely is. You are never obligated to respond instantly to any financial or security request, regardless of how urgent the attacker makes it seem.

If you accidentally clicked a link or entered information, act immediately. Change your passwords and mobile money PIN right away. Contact your mobile money provider's official customer service line (find the number on their official website, not in the suspicious message). Notify your bank if applicable. If you experience a SIM swap — your phone suddenly loses signal without explanation — call your mobile network provider immediately from another phone and report that your number may have been compromised. The faster you act, the more likely you are to limit the damage.

For reporting: contact the Liberia Telecommunications Authority (LTA) at their official hotline, and report to the Liberia National Police cybercrime unit. Document everything — screenshots of messages, phone numbers, message content, times and dates — before reporting. Sharing information about scams with family, friends, and on community WhatsApp groups is one of the most effective ways to prevent others from becoming victims. Awareness spreads faster than any government campaign when communities share actively. Your experience, if shared, could prevent someone you care about from losing their money.

💡 Stay Safe Online — Rules to Live By

  • Never share your mobile money PIN, bank PIN, or OTP code with anyone — not a bank representative, not a mobile money agent, not even a family member.
  • Before acting on any urgent financial message, call the organization directly using the official number from their website, not a number provided in the suspicious message.
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all your important accounts — email, social media, and any financial apps.
  • Regularly check your mobile money transaction history for unauthorized transactions. Report discrepancies immediately.

✅ Key Takeaways

  • Cybercriminals target ordinary Liberians through phishing, vishing, SIM swap fraud, fake job offers, and romance scams — awareness is your best defense.
  • Urgency and fear are the attacker's primary weapons — slow down, think critically, and verify before acting on any unexpected financial message.
  • Never share your PIN, password, or OTP with anyone, regardless of who they claim to be or how convincing they sound.
  • Report suspected scams to the LTA, document everything with screenshots, and share awareness in your community.
