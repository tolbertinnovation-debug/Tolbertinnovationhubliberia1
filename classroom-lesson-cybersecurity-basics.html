🔒 Cybersecurity & Online Safety

Protect your personal information, accounts, and devices from hackers, scammers, and online threats, essential knowledge for every digital citizen.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand: Common Online Threats and Scams
  • Understand: Creating and Managing Strong Passwords
  • Understand: Safe Browsing and Social Media Practices
  • Understand: Protecting Your Phone and Data

Understanding Online Threats

Cybercrime is growing rapidly in Africa. In Liberia and across West Africa, the most common threats are phishing scams, fake job offers, romance fraud, and mobile money fraud. Understanding how attacks work is the first step to protecting yourself.

Phishing is when criminals send fake emails, messages, or websites pretending to be a trusted organization (bank, NGO, government) to steal your login details or money. Signs of phishing: urgent language ("Your account will be closed in 24 hours!"), unexpected requests for passwords or payment, email addresses that look almost right but have small errors (e.g. ngo-liberia@gmail.com instead of an official domain), and links that don't match the organization's real website.

Malware is software secretly installed on your device to steal data or damage your files. It spreads through email attachments, pirated software downloads, and infected USB drives. Ransomware locks your files and demands payment to unlock them. The best protection: never open email attachments from unknown senders, only download software from official sources, and keep your operating system updated.

Creating and Managing Strong Passwords

Weak passwords are the #1 cause of account hacking. A strong password is: at least 12 characters long, includes uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols, does not use personal information (birthdate, name, phone number), and is unique to each account; never reused.

Examples of weak passwords: password123, john1990, liberia2024. Examples of strong passwords: T!h#Lib3r!a2026, M@r!ket&Day_88. An easy method: choose 4 random words and add numbers and symbols: "Purple$Rain#Book!47" is both memorable and extremely strong.

Never share your password with anyone, including people claiming to be IT support or your employer. A legitimate organization will never ask for your password. Use a different password for every important account (email, banking, social media). If you have too many to remember, use a reputable password manager app like Bitwarden (free) to store them securely.

⚠ Never Do These

  • Write passwords on paper near your computer
  • Use the same password for multiple accounts
  • Share passwords via WhatsApp or SMS
  • Log into important accounts on public computers without logging out

Safe Browsing and Social Media Safety

When browsing websites, look for the padlock icon (🔒) in your browser's address bar, meaning the connection is encrypted (HTTPS). Never enter personal information or passwords on sites that show "Not Secure" or use HTTP without S. On public Wi-Fi (cafes, offices, hotels), never access your bank account or enter sensitive information, as your data can be intercepted.

Social media safety: Set your profile to private unless you are a public figure or business. Be careful about what personal information you share. Your home address, daily routine, and financial situation should not be public. Criminals use social media to identify targets for scams and theft. Before meeting anyone you connected with online, verify their identity independently.

Common social media scams in Liberia: fake job offers that ask for "processing fees", romance scams where the person claims to be overseas and needs money, investment schemes promising huge returns, and fake prizes or lotteries. Rule: if it seems too good to be true, it is a scam.

Protecting Your Phone and Data

Your smartphone contains more personal information than anything else you own: contacts, banking apps, photos, emails, and messages. Protect it: Always use a PIN, password, or fingerprint lock. Enable "Find My Phone" so you can locate or wipe your phone if it is stolen. Back up your phone data to Google Drive or iCloud weekly. Keep your operating system and apps updated, as updates often fix security vulnerabilities.

Mobile money safety (MTN, Lonestar, Orange): Never share your PIN with anyone. Banks and mobile money providers will never call or text asking for your PIN. Be careful of "wrong transfer" scams. Someone sends you money and asks you to send it back; the original transfer may be fraudulent. Register your SIM card under your own name so it can be recovered if stolen.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on all important accounts: Gmail, Facebook, WhatsApp, banking. This means even if someone steals your password, they cannot access your account without also having your phone. Go to your account settings and look for "Security" or "Two-Step Verification" to enable this.

💡 Key Tips

  • Enable 2FA (Two-Factor Authentication) on Gmail, Facebook, and any financial app; this single step prevents 99% of account takeovers.
  • When in doubt about an email or message, do not click any links. Go directly to the official website by typing the address yourself.
  • Free Wi-Fi is not safe for banking or private logins. Use mobile data for sensitive activities.
  • Regularly check which apps have access to your location, camera, and microphone in your phone's settings and revoke access for apps that do not need it.
  • If you think you have been scammed, report it immediately. Do not be embarrassed. Contact your bank or mobile money provider within minutes.

📝 Practice Assignment

Complete a personal security audit: 1) Check the 3 most important accounts you own (email, social media, or bank): are you using a strong, unique password for each? If not, update them now. 2) Enable Two-Factor Authentication on your Gmail account (go to myaccount.google.com > Security > 2-Step Verification). 3) Review your Facebook or Instagram privacy settings: who can see your posts and personal information? Adjust to "Friends" or "Private" where appropriate. 4) Write down the 3 biggest online risks you personally face and one action you will take to address each.
