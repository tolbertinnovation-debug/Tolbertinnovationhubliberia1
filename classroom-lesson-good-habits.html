Building Good Habits for Life

You don't rise to the level of your goals — you fall to the level of your habits. Make them work for you.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain how habits form using the cue-routine-reward loop
  • Use habit stacking to attach new habits to existing ones
  • Design a morning routine that sets you up for peak performance
  • Break bad habits by understanding what needs they fulfill

🔄 How Habits Actually Work

MIT neuroscientist Ann Graybiel discovered that habits are stored in the basal ganglia — the oldest part of the brain. Once a habit is formed, it runs almost on autopilot, freeing up your conscious brain for higher thinking.

Charles Duhigg's research identified the Habit Loop — every habit has three parts:

1. Cue (Trigger)

The signal that starts the habit. Time, place, emotion, person, or preceding action.

2. Routine (Behavior)

The actual habit — the physical, mental, or emotional behavior that follows the cue.

3. Reward

The payoff that reinforces the habit and tells your brain: "Do this again."

Example — Bad habit: Cue: Stress → Routine: Scroll social media → Reward: Temporary escape/stimulation

Example — Good habit: Cue: Wake up → Routine: Drink a glass of water → Reward: Feeling alert and healthy

💡 Key insight: You cannot eliminate a bad habit — you can only replace it. Keep the same cue and reward, but swap the routine for a healthier behavior that delivers a similar reward.

🏗️ Habit Stacking — The Easiest Way to Build New Habits

Habit stacking (from James Clear's Atomic Habits) links a new habit to one you already do automatically:

Formula: "After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."

Examples for Liberian professionals:

  • "After I wake up, I will read one page of a business book before checking my phone."
  • "After I eat lunch, I will spend 10 minutes on my TIH lesson."
  • "After I finish work, I will write tomorrow's top 3 tasks before closing my phone."
  • "After I pray/church on Sunday, I will do my weekly business/career review."

The existing habit acts as the cue — you don't need to remember to start, because the trigger is already automatic.

⚠️ The "all or nothing" trap: Missing one day doesn't break a habit — missing two days in a row does. When you miss a day, your only job is to not miss the next day. Progress, not perfection.

🌅 Designing a High-Performance Morning Routine

The first 60–90 minutes of your day set the mental and emotional tone for everything that follows. Successful leaders are deliberate about this time.

A sample 60-minute morning routine:

  1. 0–5 min: Don't touch your phone. Drink water. Use the bathroom.
  2. 5–15 min: Light exercise or stretching. Even 10 push-ups changes your physiology.
  3. 15–25 min: Quiet time — prayer, meditation, journaling, or gratitude practice.
  4. 25–40 min: Learning — read, listen to a podcast, or complete a TIH lesson.
  5. 40–55 min: Review your top 3 goals and your 3 tasks for today.
  6. 55–60 min: NOW check your messages and email. Respond to urgent items only.

This routine protects your most productive mental hours from being hijacked by other people's agendas.

💡 Start small: Don't overhaul your entire morning at once. Add one new step to your existing routine and make it consistent for 3 weeks before adding another. Consistency beats intensity.

📊 Tracking Progress — Don't Break the Chain

Comedian Jerry Seinfeld's productivity secret: he marks an X on a calendar every day he writes jokes. His only goal: don't break the chain. This simple visual system is powerful.

Simple habit tracking methods:

  • Paper calendar — mark an X for each day you complete your habit. See your streak grow.
  • Habit tracking app — Habitica, Streaks, or HabitNow (all free)
  • WhatsApp accountability group — share daily check-in with 2–3 friends. Social accountability doubles follow-through rates.
  • Habit journal — a notebook where you log habits, notes, and reflections

The two-minute rule (from Atomic Habits): Any new habit should take less than two minutes to start. "Read every night" becomes "open my book." "Exercise daily" becomes "put on my trainers." Starting is the hardest part — make it effortless.
