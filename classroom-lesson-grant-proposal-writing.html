📋 Writing a Compelling Grant Proposal

Every year, millions of dollars in grant funding goes to organizations in Liberia and across West Africa — but most of it goes to the same few organizations that know how to write proposals that win. This lesson teaches you the proven structure, the language, and the techniques that make funders say yes to your project rather than filing your proposal in the rejection pile.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand: The standard structure of a professional grant proposal
  • Understand: How to write a problem statement that connects with funders' priorities
  • Understand: How to write SMART project objectives for Liberian development contexts
  • Understand: How to build an impact narrative that demonstrates lasting change

What Makes Funders Say Yes (and No)

Funders — whether international donors (USAID, UNDP, EU), foundations (Ford Foundation, Gates Foundation), or government grant programs — receive hundreds of proposals for every grant cycle. They cannot fund every good idea. What separates the proposals that succeed from those that fail is rarely the quality of the underlying project: it is the quality of the writing and the discipline with which the proposal responds to the funder's stated priorities. A funder says yes when the problem is clearly defined with evidence, the proposed solution is logical and feasible, the budget is realistic and clearly justified, the organization has demonstrated competence, and the expected impact is concrete and measurable.

A funder says no when the problem is vague ("youth face challenges in Liberia"), the objectives are unmeasurable ("improve youth skills"), the budget has unexplained line items, the proposal is poorly written with grammar errors, or the project does not align with the funder's stated priorities. Many strong projects are rejected simply because the proposal writer did not read the guidelines carefully enough — they wrote about what they wanted to do rather than demonstrating how their project fits the funder's strategy. The proposal is not just a description of your project; it is an argument to a specific audience for a specific purpose.

The most important thing to understand before writing any proposal is: read the funder's guidelines and strategic priorities carefully — ideally three times. Your project must align with what THEY want to fund, not just what you want to do. If a funder's current priority is climate resilience in coastal communities and your project is urban youth vocational training, do not apply — or do not apply without clearly showing how your project connects to their climate goals. Every sentence you write should be answering a question the funder has asked, not a question you invented.

The Core Structure of a Grant Proposal

Every grant proposal, whether 5 pages or 50 pages, contains the same core components. Understanding this structure means you are never starting from a blank page — you are filling in a known framework with evidence and compelling language. The seven sections below form the backbone of any professional proposal. Some funders will ask for these sections in a specific order or with specific word limits; always follow their template exactly. But even when the format varies, these elements must be present.

  1. 1.Executive Summary (1 page maximum) — A concise overview of the problem, your solution, who benefits, the total budget, and the project timeframe. Many funders and reviewers read only this section before deciding whether to continue. It must be compelling and complete enough to stand alone.
  2. 2.Problem Statement — Describes the issue you are addressing, supported with evidence and data from credible sources. Explicitly connects the problem to the funder's stated priorities and demonstrates why this problem requires external funding to solve.
  3. 3.Project Objectives — Specific, measurable goals your project will achieve within the project period. These are the commitments you are making to the funder — they will evaluate your success against these objectives.
  4. 4.Activities and Timeline — What you will actually do, when you will do it, and in what sequence. This section shows funders that your plan is realistic and that you have thought through the implementation carefully.
  5. 5.Budget — A detailed breakdown of all costs, clearly justified and aligned with your activities. Every line item must make sense: funders should never wonder what a cost is for or why it is that amount.
  6. 6.Organizational Background — Who you are, your track record of delivering similar work, and why your organization is qualified and trustworthy to implement this project. Include past projects, beneficiary numbers, and any relevant partnerships.
  7. 7.Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) — How you will measure success, collect data, and report results to the funder. Funders need confidence that you will know whether your project worked and that you will be accountable to them throughout the project period.

Writing a Powerful Problem Statement

The problem statement is the heart of your proposal. If the funder does not believe the problem is serious, real, and urgent, they will not fund your solution — no matter how well designed it is. Your job in this section is to make the funder feel the weight of the problem as clearly and concretely as possible. The single most effective tool for doing this is data: quote specific statistics from credible sources. For example: "According to the Liberia Labor Force Survey 2022, youth unemployment in Montserrado County stands at 47%, with over 60% of unemployed youth lacking any formal vocational skills." Numbers anchor the problem in reality and signal to the funder that you have done your research.

Beyond statistics, connect the problem to real people by using a brief, specific description that makes the issue human and tangible. For example: "In Paynesville, young women between 18 and 25 who did not complete secondary school have virtually no access to formal employment, forcing many into informal hawking with extremely low and unpredictable incomes that cannot cover household needs for food, school fees, or healthcare." This kind of description does not replace data — it works alongside data to create a complete picture that moves reviewers emotionally as well as intellectually. Funders give money to problems they understand and care about.

Finally, connect the problem explicitly to the funder's own priorities. If the funder focuses on gender equity, show how the problem disproportionately affects women and girls. If they focus on economic development, show the economic cost of the problem to the community or country. If they focus on health, show the health consequences of the issue. This direct connection is not manipulation — it is relevance, and relevance is what grants are awarded on. Avoid vague language at all costs. "Many youth face challenges" is weak and tells the funder nothing. "47% of youth aged 18–30 in urban Liberia are unemployed, representing a substantial and measurable drag on household incomes and national economic productivity" is strong, specific, and fundable.

Writing SMART Objectives

Objectives are the specific outcomes your project will achieve. They are the promises you make to the funder — and they will be used to evaluate whether your project succeeded. Objectives must be SMART: Specific (exactly what will happen and for whom), Measurable (expressed as a number, percentage, or verifiable outcome), Achievable (realistic given your budget, timeline, and organizational capacity), Relevant (directly addresses the problem you described), and Time-bound (states clearly by when the outcome will be achieved).

The difference between a weak objective and a SMART objective is the difference between a proposal that gets rejected and one that gets funded. A weak objective like "improve youth employment opportunities" is impossible to measure and gives the funder no confidence that you know what you are actually going to do. A SMART objective like "By December 2025, train 120 unemployed youth aged 18–30 in Paynesville in digital marketing skills, with at least 70% completing certification and 40% securing income-generating opportunities within 6 months of training" tells the funder exactly who you are serving, what they will learn, how many will complete, and what employment outcome you expect — and it gives them clear metrics to hold you accountable to.

SMART Component Example from a Liberian NGO Project
Specific "Train 120 youth in digital marketing in Paynesville"
Measurable "70% completion rate; 40% securing income-generating employment within 6 months"
Achievable "Based on existing trainer capacity, rented facility, and established community partnerships"
Relevant "Directly addresses 47% youth unemployment rate in target community"
Time-bound "Training cohorts complete by December 2025; employment follow-up by June 2026"

Activities, Timeline, and the Impact Narrative

Your activities section answers the question "how?" — it lists every major action needed to achieve each objective in a logical, sequenced order. For each activity, identify who is responsible, what inputs are required, and how long it will take. Pair your activities with a clear timeline. A simple Gantt-chart approach works well in grant proposals: list months (Month 1, Month 2, and so on) across the top of a table and activities down the side, marking with an X or shading which months each activity occurs. Key milestones — such as "training begins," "first cohort graduates," "M&E midpoint review," and "final report submitted" — should be clearly marked so funders can see the project's rhythm and pacing at a glance.

The impact narrative goes beyond objectives to describe the lasting change your project will create — not just during the project period, but in the years after funding ends. Think through multiple levels of change: who benefits directly (120 youth trained in digital skills), who benefits indirectly (their families, future employers who gain skilled workers, local businesses that gain customers with higher incomes), what changes in the longer term (increased household income in target area, reduced youth unemployment rate, stronger local digital economy), and whether the change is sustainable without continued external funding. Funders increasingly want to see that their investment creates change that outlasts the grant, not just a project that stops when money runs out.

Your theory of change is the logical chain that makes your impact narrative credible and testable. State it explicitly in your proposal: IF we provide digital skills training to unemployed youth in Paynesville, AND IF we connect graduates to employers and freelance opportunities through our network, THEN youth unemployment decreases and household income rises in the target community, BECAUSE youth now have marketable skills that local and regional employers actively demand. Every word of that chain must be defensible — the "because" is where funders and evaluators will probe hardest, and you must have evidence that the logical connection between your activities and your intended impact is real.

💡 Grant Writing Pro Tips

  • Always read the Request for Proposals (RFP) or grant guidelines at least three times before writing a single word. Everything you write must respond directly to their specific requirements, evaluation criteria, and priorities.
  • Use sub-headings that mirror the funder's own language and section structure from their guidelines — this makes it easy for reviewers to find what they need and signals that you read their documents carefully.
  • Get a colleague who was NOT involved in writing the proposal to read the problem statement cold. If they understand the problem clearly after one read, it is well written. If they are confused, rewrite it before submitting.
  • Build a proposal file with reusable sections — organizational background, standard M&E frameworks, staff CVs, common budget line items — so you can respond quickly to future calls for proposals without starting from scratch every time.

✅ Key Takeaways

  • A winning proposal addresses the funder's priorities with clear evidence and logical alignment — not just your organization's needs and interests.
  • The Problem Statement is the most critical section of any proposal — use data, real human examples, and an explicit connection to the funder's stated priorities.
  • SMART objectives give funders the confidence that you know exactly what you will achieve, how you will measure it, and that you will be accountable for results.
  • Your impact narrative must show not just what you will do during the project, but what lasting change it will create, for whom, and why that change will persist after funding ends.

📝 Practical Assignment

Write a 150–200 word problem statement for a hypothetical project in your community. You may choose any issue: youth skills training, clean water access, women's economic empowerment, health education, school infrastructure, or another issue you care about. Your problem statement must: (1) identify the specific problem with at least one statistic or concrete piece of evidence, (2) describe who is affected and how their lives are impacted, (3) explain why this problem needs addressing now. You can find Liberia statistics at the Liberia Institute of Statistics and Geo-Information Services website (lisgis.net) or by searching "Liberia [topic] statistics." Share your problem statement in the TIH WhatsApp group for peer feedback.
