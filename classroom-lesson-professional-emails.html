Why Professional Emails Matter

Your email is often the first impression a potential employer, client, or colleague has of you. A poorly written email with spelling mistakes, no subject line, or an unprofessional tone can cost you a job or business deal before you even meet someone. Professional emails are clear, concise, correctly spelled, politely toned, and properly formatted. They get read and responded to.

The Structure of a Professional Email

Every professional email has five parts: (1) Subject Line: Short, specific, informative. "Job Application - Accountant Position" is better than "Hello". (2) Greeting: "Dear Mr. Kollie," or "Dear Hiring Manager," - always use the name if you know it. (3) Body: State your purpose in the first sentence. Be clear and direct. (4) Closing: "Kind regards," or "Sincerely," - never "Bye" or "Thanks alot". (5) Signature: Your full name, title, organization, phone number, and email.

Tone, Language, and Common Mistakes

Be polite and professional even if you are frustrated. Avoid slang and abbreviations. Common mistakes: no subject line (your email may be ignored), starting with "I" (try "Following up on..." instead), emails that are too long (if it needs more than 200 words, consider a meeting instead), forgetting to attach the file you mentioned in the body.

Sample Professional Email Format

Subject: Application for Administrative Assistant Position - June 2026. Dear Ms. Weah, I am writing to apply for the Administrative Assistant position advertised on your website. I hold a diploma in Business Administration from the University of Liberia and have three years of office experience. Please find my CV attached for your review. I am available for an interview at your convenience. Kind regards, Emmanuel Kollie, +231 880 000 000, e.kollie@email.com

💡 Key Tips

  • Proofread every email before sending - read it aloud to catch awkward phrasing.
  • Use a professional email address (firstname.lastname@gmail.com) not nicknames.
  • Respond to emails within 24-48 hours when possible.
  • Never write an email when you are angry - draft it, wait an hour, then review.

📝 Practice Assignment

Write a professional email applying for a job or internship you would genuinely like. Include: (1) A specific subject line. (2) A proper greeting. (3) A 3-paragraph body: who you are, why you are applying, what you attach. (4) A professional closing and email signature. Ask a friend or mentor to review your email for tone and clarity.
