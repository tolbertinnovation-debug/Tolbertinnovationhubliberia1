👥 Workplace Communication & Meetings

Communicate clearly with colleagues and managers, run effective meetings, handle difficult conversations, and build a reputation as a reliable professional.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand: Professional Communication Styles at Work
  • Understand: Running and Participating in Productive Meetings
  • Understand: Handling Difficult Conversations
  • Understand: Giving and Receiving Feedback Professionally

Professional Communication at Work

Workplace communication is different from casual conversation with friends. It requires clarity, respect, and professionalism regardless of your relationship with the other person. The key communication mistake most professionals make is being unclear — assuming the other person knows what you mean when you have not said it directly.

The Four Cs of Professional Communication: Clear (say exactly what you mean), Concise (no unnecessary words), Correct (accurate information), and Courteous (respectful tone). When communicating with a manager or superior: always be direct but respectful. Lead with the conclusion, then the details. "The report will be late by 2 days because the data was not received on time" is better than a long explanation that buries the key information.

Non-verbal communication matters as much as words in face-to-face settings. Maintain appropriate eye contact (looking away too much signals discomfort or dishonesty). Keep a calm, open posture — arms uncrossed, body facing the speaker. Avoid looking at your phone when someone is speaking to you. In Liberian workplace culture, respect for hierarchy is important; address senior colleagues formally until invited to use first names.

Running and Participating in Productive Meetings

Poor meetings are one of the biggest time wasters in any organization. Research shows that 67% of meetings are considered unproductive by participants. The difference between good and bad meetings is preparation and structure. Before a meeting: define the purpose (what decision needs to be made?), share an agenda in advance, invite only people who need to be there, and set a specific end time.

During a meeting: start on time — waiting for latecomers punishes those who arrived punctually. Designate someone to take minutes (written notes). Keep discussions focused on the agenda. For each agenda item, aim to reach a clear decision or next action. End by summarizing what was decided and who is responsible for each action item, with deadlines.

As a participant: review the agenda before attending. Come prepared with relevant information or opinions. Speak up — silent participants are often perceived as disengaged. Keep contributions concise and relevant. If a discussion drifts off-topic, it is acceptable to say: "Can we add that to the next agenda and return to the main point?" After the meeting: complete your assigned actions by the stated deadline. This alone distinguishes reliable professionals from unreliable ones.

Handling Difficult Conversations

Every professional eventually needs to address uncomfortable topics — a missed deadline, poor performance, a disagreement with a colleague, or a complaint from a client. Avoiding difficult conversations creates bigger problems over time. The key is to have them early, privately, and constructively.

The SBI Framework (Situation-Behavior-Impact) makes difficult conversations structured and less emotional. Situation: describe the specific event objectively. Behavior: describe the specific behavior you observed. Impact: explain the impact that behavior had. Example: "During the client meeting on Monday (Situation), you interrupted the client three times before they finished speaking (Behavior), which made them visibly frustrated and they shortened the meeting (Impact)."

Rules for difficult conversations: choose a private setting, not in front of colleagues. Focus on behaviors and outcomes, not personality or character. Listen to the other person's perspective — they may have information you do not. Seek a solution, not just an expression of frustration. End with a clear agreement on what changes are expected. Follow up to acknowledge improvement or address recurring issues.

Giving and Receiving Feedback

Feedback is how professionals grow. The best organizations have a culture of regular, honest feedback. The most effective feedback model is the "sandwich" approach: start with something positive (specific), give the constructive criticism (specific and actionable), end with encouragement or another positive. Example: "Your presentation was well-structured and the data was compelling. The delivery could be improved by maintaining more eye contact with the audience. Overall, this was one of your strongest presentations yet."

When receiving feedback: listen without interrupting. Resist the urge to defend yourself immediately. Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?" Thank the person for the feedback — even if it stings. Reflect before responding. Separate the feedback from your ego — criticism of your work is not criticism of your worth as a person. Identify one specific thing you can improve based on the feedback and follow through.

Upward feedback (giving feedback to a manager): this requires tact. Frame it as observations and questions rather than criticism. "I noticed that decisions sometimes come late in the week, which makes it difficult to complete tasks by Friday. Would it be possible to set a Thursday deadline for approvals?" This proposes a solution while identifying the problem diplomatically.

💡 Key Tips

  • Send a meeting agenda at least 24 hours in advance — people who come prepared make every meeting 50% shorter.
  • After any meeting, send a brief follow-up email summarizing decisions and action items — this creates accountability and prevents misunderstandings.
  • Address problems early — a 5-minute conversation now prevents a 5-hour crisis later.
  • Use "I" statements in difficult conversations instead of "you" — "I felt excluded when I wasn't copied on the email" is less confrontational than "You excluded me."
  • The best communicators listen twice as much as they speak — they ask questions and genuinely try to understand before responding.

📝 Practice Assignment

Reflect on a recent difficult conversation you avoided, or one you handled poorly. Using the SBI framework, rewrite how you would approach that conversation professionally. Then: plan a mock meeting agenda on any topic (team project, community event, etc.) with a clear purpose, 4 agenda items, designated roles, and a time limit. Practice running the meeting with friends or family, or write out how the meeting should proceed step by step. Write a meeting follow-up email summarizing the decisions and action items from your mock meeting.
